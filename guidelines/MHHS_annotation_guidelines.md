# Mental Health Hope Speech (MH-HS) Annotation Guidelines

## 1. Introduction

These guidelines define the annotation criteria for Mental Health Hope Speech (MH-HS) in depression-related social media discourse.

Unlike traditional Hope Speech (HS) definitions developed in Equality, Diversity, and Inclusion (EDI) contexts, MH-HS focuses on linguistic expressions associated with:
- adaptive coping,
- recovery orientation,
- emotional resilience,
- interpersonal support,
- and future-directed self-efficacy
in contexts of emotional distress.

The purpose of MH-HS annotation is not to identify generic positivity, but rather discourse reflecting psychologically meaningful coping-oriented communication.

---

# 2. Definition of MH-HS

A message should be labeled as MH-HS when it contains discourse reflecting at least one of the following:

- adaptive coping orientation,
- recovery-oriented thinking,
- resilience under distress,
- emotional perseverance,
- future-oriented self-efficacy,
- constructive interpersonal support,
- or psychologically meaningful reframing of adversity.

Importantly:
- MH-HS may coexist with sadness, anxiety, hopelessness, or emotional suffering.
- MH-HS is NOT equivalent to positive sentiment.
- MH-HS is NOT simply emotional support or empathy.

The focus is on:
## constructive coping orientation under distress.

---

# 3. Annotation Task

Annotators must assign:

## 3.1 Binary MH-HS Label

- 1 → MH-HS present
- 0 → MH-HS absent

---

## 3.2 Optional MH-HS Dimensions

A message may express one or more MH-HS dimensions:

| Dimension | Description |
|---|---|
| Agency | Expressions of effort, persistence, self-efficacy, or intentional coping |
| Recovery | References to improvement, healing, recovery, or progress |
| Resilience | Emotional endurance despite suffering |
| Support | Constructive emotional or interpersonal support |
| Future Orientation | Future-directed hope or possibility of improvement |
| Reframing | Adaptive reinterpretation of adversity |

Multiple dimensions may coexist.

---

# 4. General Annotation Principles

## 4.1 Context Matters

Messages should be interpreted considering:
- conversational context,
- previous messages,
- emotional framing,
- and subject-level discourse when available.

Some messages may only express MH-HS when interpreted contextually.

---

## 4.2 Emotional Negativity Does NOT Exclude MH-HS

A message may contain:
- sadness,
- depression,
- anxiety,
- frustration,
- emotional pain,
while still expressing adaptive coping.

Example:

> “I still feel terrible, but I’m trying to keep moving forward.”

This SHOULD be labeled as MH-HS.

---

## 4.3 Generic Positivity Is Not Necessarily MH-HS

Simple positive expressions without coping orientation should NOT automatically receive MH-HS labels.

Example:

> “Everything will be amazing :)”

This alone is NOT sufficient.

---

## 4.4 Toxic Positivity Should NOT Be Labeled as MH-HS

Messages invalidating emotional suffering through unrealistic positivity should NOT be considered MH-HS.

Example:

> “Just stop being sad and think positively.”

This is NOT MH-HS.

---

# 5. Positive MH-HS Examples

## Example 1 — Recovery Orientation

> “I know recovery is slow, but I’m improving little by little.”

Label:
- MH-HS = 1
- Dimensions:
  - Recovery
  - Future Orientation

---

## Example 2 — Emotional Resilience

> “Some days are horrible, but I’m still here fighting.”

Label:
- MH-HS = 1
- Dimensions:
  - Resilience
  - Agency

---

## Example 3 — Supportive Coping

> “You’re not alone in this. Seeking help was important for me too.”

Label:
- MH-HS = 1
- Dimensions:
  - Support
  - Recovery

---

## Example 4 — Adaptive Reframing

> “Therapy hasn’t solved everything, but it helped me understand myself better.”

Label:
- MH-HS = 1
- Dimensions:
  - Reframing
  - Recovery

---

Label:
- MH-HS = 0

Label:
- MH-HS = 0

---

## Example 3 — Generic Positivity

Label:
- MH-HS = 0

---

## Example 4 — Toxic Positivity

> “Depression is just a mindset.”

Label:
- MH-HS = 0

---

# 7. Borderline Cases

Borderline cases should be discussed during adjudication.

Special attention should be given to:
- irony,
- sarcasm,
- indirect coping expressions,
- ambiguous motivational language,
- and context-dependent interpretations.

When uncertainty exists, annotators should prioritize:
## observable coping orientation
rather than emotional tone alone.

---

# 8. Annotation Procedure

Annotators should:
1. Read the target message.
2. Review available contextual messages if needed.
3. Assign the binary MH-HS label.
4. Assign optional MH-HS dimensions.
5. Add notes for ambiguous cases.

---

# 9. Important Clarifications

MH-HS is:
- NOT a diagnosis,
- NOT a direct measurement of psychological hope,
- NOT equivalent to positive emotion,
- NOT equivalent to sentiment polarity.

MH-HS is instead:
## a discourse-level indicator of adaptive coping orientation.

---

# 10. Ethical Considerations

Annotations involve sensitive mental health-related discourse.

Annotators should:
- avoid overinterpretation,
- avoid diagnostic assumptions,
- and prioritize respectful contextual interpretation.

The resulting labels should never be interpreted as clinical judgments.
